# SANGUINE — Hot & Moist

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Sanguine body is governed by blood and the airy principle. Ptolemy associates the native born under a predominantly moist and warm constitution with a well-favored complexion, good color in the skin, robustness of frame, and large, bright eyes. The body tends to be tall and commanding — the warmth promotes growth and the moisture gives flesh and good color. Lilly describes the Sanguine complexion as fair or clear, and the disposition as cheerful, liberal, and affectionate.

The primary health susceptibility of the Sanguine temperament is excess — the warmth and moisture together tend to produce too much blood, and when this accumulates without proper circulation and expenditure, the native may be prone to feverish conditions, inflammation, headaches, and disorders of the blood and circulatory system. The heart, arteries, and lungs are the organs most associated with this temperament through the governance of Jupiter, who shares the Sanguine nature. Disorders tend to be acute rather than chronic — coming on strongly and passing quickly when properly addressed.

*"The quadrant from the spring equinox to the summer solstice makes the subjects well-favoured in complexion, stature, robustness, and eyes, and exceeding in the moist and warm."*
— Claudius Ptolemy, Tetrabiblos, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**The governing principle:** The Sanguine native benefits most from what moderates excess warmth and moisture without suppressing the natural vitality that makes this temperament so pleasant. The aim is to channel the abundant natural energy rather than to restrict it.

**Climate and season:** The Sanguine native flourishes in cool, dry conditions that provide a natural counterbalance to the excess warmth and moisture. Autumn is the most beneficial season — its cold and dry qualities counteract the Sanguine excess. Hot and humid climates should be moderated when possible, as they compound the native's natural tendency toward excess blood and heat.

**Diet:** Foods that are cooling and moderately drying are beneficial — bitter greens, astringent fruits, and foods with a drying quality. Rich, heavy, and excessively sweet foods compound the Sanguine excess and should be taken in moderation. Wine, taken moderately, is not harmful to the Sanguine native, but excess must be avoided as it compounds the blood.

**Exercise and activity:** The Sanguine native has naturally abundant physical energy and benefits greatly from regular vigorous exercise that promotes circulation and spends the excess blood productively. Without regular physical expenditure, the warmth and moisture accumulate and begin to cause the feverish and inflammatory conditions to which this temperament is susceptible. Lilly notes that the Sanguine nature is cheerful and social — activities that combine physical engagement with pleasurable social connection serve this native best.

**Cautions:** The primary caution for the Sanguine native is overindulgence — in food, drink, pleasure, and social engagement. The natural ease and cheerfulness of this temperament can lead to excess in all pleasurable things, and the accumulated warmth and moisture eventually seek outlet through fever, inflammation, or skin conditions.

# CHOLERIC — Hot & Dry

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Choleric body is governed by yellow bile and the fiery principle. Ptolemy associates the native born under a predominantly warm and dry constitution with a ruddy or florid complexion, a tall and robust frame, and a vigorous, intense physical presence. The body tends toward leanness rather than flesh — the dryness contracts and firms, and the heat burns away excess moisture. Lilly describes the Choleric complexion as yellow or red, and notes that the Choleric native is bold, ambitious, and prone to anger and impatience.

The primary health susceptibility of the Choleric temperament is the inflammation produced by excess heat and dryness — fevers of the sharp, burning kind, disorders of the bile, and inflammatory skin conditions. Mars, who governs this temperament most directly, rules the kidneys, veins, and genitals, and Ptolemy notes that Mars causes men to spit blood, weakens the lungs, and produces conditions requiring cutting or cautery. The Choleric native tends toward acute, intense episodes of illness rather than chronic conditions — the fiery nature burns intensely but does not linger.

*"The quadrant from the summer solstice to the autumn equinox produces individuals with moderately good complexion and moderate height, robust, with large eyes and thick and curly hair, exceeding in the warm and dry."*
— Claudius Ptolemy, Tetrabiblos, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**The governing principle:** The Choleric native benefits most from what cools and moistens — the two qualities most opposed to their natural excess. The aim is to moderate the fire and dryness without extinguishing the genuine vitality and courage that characterize this temperament at its best.

**Climate and season:** The Choleric native flourishes in cool, moist conditions. Winter and early spring, with their cold and moist qualities, provide the most natural relief from the Choleric excess. Hot and dry climates should be avoided when possible, or moderated with adequate hydration and rest in the heat of the day. The Choleric native often finds that their sharpest and most irritable moods coincide with the hottest and driest periods of the year.

**Diet:** Cooling, moist, and somewhat sweet foods are beneficial — fruits with high water content, cooling herbs, dairy products in moderation, and foods that are boiled or steamed rather than roasted. Spicy, pungent, and heavily seasoned foods compound the Choleric excess and should be taken sparingly. The Choleric native tends toward impatience in eating as in all things — deliberate, unhurried meals taken in pleasant company serve this temperament better than eating on the move.

**Exercise and activity:** The Choleric native has an intense and combative natural energy that benefits from structured physical outlets — competitive sports, martial arts, or any vigorous physical discipline that provides a legitimate channel for the natural aggression. Without such outlets, the heat tends to discharge through anger and conflict in social and professional life. Lilly notes the Choleric native's natural boldness and ambition — channeling these qualities into genuine achievement is itself a form of health.

**Cautions:** The primary cautions for the Choleric native are anger, excess heat, and inflammation in all its forms. A quick temper, if left unchecked, generates internal heat that compounds the natural Choleric tendency. Sleep, taken in adequate quantity and in cool conditions, is among the most effective remedies available to this temperament.

# PHLEGMATIC — Cold & Moist

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Phlegmatic body is governed by phlegm and the watery principle. Ptolemy associates the native born under a predominantly cold and moist constitution with a pale complexion, a tendency toward softness and roundness of form, and a more graceful than robust physical presence. The body tends toward pallor — the cold reduces vitality and color, and the moisture softens and relaxes the tissues. Lilly describes the Phlegmatic complexion as pale, and notes that the Phlegmatic native tends toward timidity, mutability, and an inclination to take the shape of surrounding circumstances.

The primary health susceptibility of the Phlegmatic temperament is accumulation of cold and moisture — conditions characterized by excess fluid, sluggish digestion, respiratory congestion, and a general tendency toward what Ptolemy and the humoral tradition call "cold and damp diseases." Saturn, who shares the Phlegmatic quality, governs the spleen, bladder, and bones, and Ptolemy notes that Saturn causes cold bellies, increases phlegm, produces rheumatic conditions, jaundice, and colic. The Phlegmatic native tends toward chronic, slow-developing conditions rather than acute episodes — the coldness and moisture accumulate gradually and are slow to clear.

*"Saturn causes his subjects to have cold bellies, increases the phlegm, makes them rheumatic, meagre, weak, jaundiced, and prone to dysentery, coughing, raising, colic, and elephantiasis."*
— Claudius Ptolemy, Tetrabiblos, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**The governing principle:** The Phlegmatic native benefits most from what warms and dries — the two qualities most opposed to their natural excess. The aim is to stimulate the natural vitality and promote the circulation and digestion that the cold and moisture tend to suppress.

**Climate and season:** The Phlegmatic native flourishes in warm, dry conditions. Summer and early autumn, with their warming and drying qualities, provide the most natural relief from the Phlegmatic excess. Cold, damp climates are particularly harmful to this temperament and should be avoided or remedied with adequate warmth, dry bedding, and avoidance of prolonged exposure to cold and wet conditions.

**Diet:** Warming, drying, and easily digested foods are most beneficial — spices, warming herbs, roasted or grilled preparations, bitter digestive foods, and foods that promote circulation and warmth. Cold, raw, and excessively moist foods compound the Phlegmatic excess. The Phlegmatic native benefits from eating warm foods at regular intervals and from avoiding the excessive consumption of cold drinks and heavy, watery foods that compound the naturally sluggish digestion.

**Exercise and activity:** The Phlegmatic native tends naturally toward rest and inactivity — which, while pleasant, compounds the cold and moist accumulation. Regular, consistent exercise that promotes warmth and circulation is among the most important practical recommendations for this temperament. Lilly notes the Phlegmatic tendency toward sluggishness in performing any business — the antidote is regular, structured activity that counteracts the natural tendency to yield to comfort and rest.

**Cautions:** The primary cautions for the Phlegmatic native are cold, dampness, excessive rest, and the consumption of cold and heavy foods. Morning exercise in dry, warm conditions, combined with a warming diet, addresses the root tendencies of this temperament more effectively than any other single practice.

# MELANCHOLIC — Cold & Dry

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Melancholic body is governed by black bile and the earthy principle. Ptolemy associates the native born under a predominantly cold and dry constitution with a sallow or dark complexion, a lean and spare frame, a somewhat sickly appearance, and a moderately good eye. The body tends toward thinness and darkness — the cold contracts and the dry hardens, producing a native of endurance and solidity but prone to the ailments of restriction and desiccation. Lilly describes the Melancholic complexion as black or dark, and notes that the Melancholic native tends toward slowness, suspicion, sorrow, and a long memory for injuries.

The primary health susceptibility of the Melancholic temperament is the accumulation of cold and dry black bile — conditions characterized by despondency, chronic pain, digestive disorders, and diseases that build slowly and resist quick resolution. Saturn, who governs this temperament most directly in its dry form, rules the spleen, bladder, and bones. Ptolemy notes that the autumn quadrant produces sallow, spare, slender, and sickly individuals — the most characteristic description of the Melancholic constitution. The Melancholic native tends toward chronic, enduring conditions rather than acute episodes, and their illnesses tend to be difficult to cure quickly.

*"The quadrant from the autumn equinox to the winter solstice makes them sallow, spare, slender, sickly, with moderately curling hair and good eyes, exceeding in the dry and cold."*
— Claudius Ptolemy, Tetrabiblos, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**The governing principle:** The Melancholic native benefits most from what warms and moistens — the two qualities most opposed to their natural excess. The aim is to soften the hardness and restriction of the cold and dry without suppressing the genuine depth and endurance that characterize this temperament at its best.

**Climate and season:** The Melancholic native flourishes in warm, moist conditions. Spring, with its warming and moistening qualities, is the most naturally beneficial season for this temperament. Cold, dry climates compound the Melancholic excess, and prolonged exposure to cold and dry conditions tends to deepen the characteristic despondency and restrict the vital functions further.

**Diet:** Warming, moist, and nourishing foods are most beneficial — broths, soups, warm and mildly sweet preparations, easily digested proteins, and foods that promote warmth and the generation of good blood. Cold, dry, and astringent foods compound the Melancholic excess. The Melancholic native often loses appetite in times of despondency — regular, nourishing meals taken in pleasant circumstances are a genuine form of medicine for this temperament.

**Exercise and activity:** The Melancholic native benefits from moderate, regular physical activity that promotes warmth and circulation without exhausting the naturally limited vital energy. The characteristic Melancholic depth of thought benefits from being balanced by physical engagement with the world — walking, gardening, or any activity that combines gentle physical movement with contact with the natural world. Lilly notes the Melancholic tendency toward suspicion and sorrow — social engagement, in moderate and trusted company, provides the moistening counterbalance to the naturally dry and withdrawn Melancholic temperament.

**Cautions:** The primary cautions for the Melancholic native are isolation, cold, dryness, and excessive rumination. The deepening spiral of Melancholic despondency is best interrupted by warmth — in food, in climate, in social engagement, and in the deliberate cultivation of pleasurable and nourishing experiences that counteract the natural tendency toward cold withdrawal.

# SANGUINE-CHOLERIC — Hot & Moist with Hot & Dry

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Sanguine-Choleric temperament is governed by an excess of heat — both the moist heat of blood and the dry heat of bile contribute to a constitution of unusual warmth and vitality. The body tends toward robustness, good color, and physical energy. The primary health susceptibility is the compound effect of heat in two forms — feverish conditions that involve both inflammation and dryness, disorders of the blood and bile together, and a tendency toward conditions that burn intensely. Lilly notes that the Sanguine-Choleric combination produces a nature that is bold, socially adept, and quick to both enthusiasm and anger.

*"If Heat and Moisture overcome, the Native is of Sanguine Complexion — if Heat and Driness, then Cholerick: where both are present in force, the native exceeds in warmth above all other constitutions."*
— William Lilly, Christian Astrology, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**Governing principle:** Moderate both the moist heat of the Sanguine and the dry heat of the Choleric by cooling and providing adequate moisture. Avoid excess in all pleasurable things, as both temperaments tend toward indulgence.

**Climate:** Cool and moderately moist conditions are most beneficial. Avoid hot and dry climates entirely. Spring and early autumn provide the best balance.

**Diet:** Cooling, moist, slightly bitter foods. Avoid heavily spiced, roasted, or rich preparations. Adequate hydration is essential — both the Sanguine and Choleric tendencies toward excess heat increase the need for moisture.

**Exercise:** Regular vigorous exercise that spends excess energy productively, but without competitive intensity that inflames the already active Choleric element. Swimming and water-based activities provide natural cooling alongside the physical expenditure.

**Cautions:** Anger and overindulgence are the twin dangers of this combination. The Sanguine element encourages excess in pleasure and the Choleric element adds intensity — together they can produce inflammatory conditions when either or both are unchecked.

# SANGUINE-PHLEGMATIC — Hot & Moist with Cold & Moist

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Sanguine-Phlegmatic temperament is governed by an excess of moisture — both the warm moisture of blood and the cold moisture of phlegm produce a constitution of unusual fluidity and adaptability. The body tends toward softness and good proportion, with a pleasant complexion that reflects the dominance of moist qualities. The primary health susceptibility is excess accumulation of fluid — the two forms of moisture together can produce conditions of fluid retention, swelling, respiratory congestion, and a general tendency toward sluggishness when the Phlegmatic element is not counteracted by the warmth of the Sanguine. Lilly notes this combination produces a native of genuine social warmth combined with emotional flexibility.

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**Governing principle:** Both elements share moisture — the primary need is for drying and toning, while maintaining the warmth provided by the Sanguine element. Avoid anything that increases dampness or cold.

**Climate:** Warm and dry conditions are most beneficial. The combination of Sanguine warmth and Phlegmatic cold produces a native who can tolerate varying conditions but benefits most from the clarity of warm, dry weather.

**Diet:** Warming, drying, and easily digested foods. Avoid cold, heavy, and excessively moist preparations. Bitter and astringent foods help counteract the excess moisture of this combination.

**Exercise:** Regular moderate exercise that promotes warmth and circulation without exhaustion. The Sanguine element provides natural energy that, when cultivated, counteracts the Phlegmatic tendency toward rest and accumulation.

**Cautions:** Excess moisture in all its forms — damp climates, cold and watery foods, inadequate exercise, and prolonged periods of rest. The cheerfulness of the Sanguine element can mask the creeping accumulation of Phlegmatic cold and moisture until symptoms become pronounced.

# SANGUINE-MELANCHOLIC — Hot & Moist with Cold & Dry

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Sanguine-Melancholic temperament combines two opposing pairs of qualities — the warm moisture of the Sanguine and the cold dryness of the Melancholic. This produces a constitution of unusual complexity: the native may alternate between periods of warmth and social engagement and periods of withdrawal and despondency. The body reflects this duality — periods of good health and vitality alternate with periods of the characteristic Melancholic susceptibilities. Lilly considers this the most intellectually productive of the mixed temperaments: "A Sanguine-Melancholic man makes the best student" — the Sanguine element provides the social warmth and enthusiasm for ideas, and the Melancholic element provides the depth, patience, and capacity for sustained concentration.

*"A Sanguine-melancholly man, makes the best Student."*
— William Lilly, Christian Astrology, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**Governing principle:** The opposing qualities of this combination require balance rather than consistent suppression of one element. The native benefits from attending to which quality is currently dominant and counteracting accordingly.

**Climate:** Temperate conditions — neither too hot and moist nor too cold and dry. Spring and early autumn are the most naturally beneficial seasons for this temperament.

**Diet:** A varied diet that avoids both extremes — neither excessively warming and stimulating (which inflames the Sanguine element) nor cold and heavy (which deepens the Melancholic element). Regular, nourishing meals taken in pleasant company support the best expression of this combination.

**Exercise:** Regular, moderate exercise that maintains warmth and circulation without exhausting the limited vital energy of the Melancholic element. The native benefits from intellectual activity as much as physical — the combination of bodily movement with intellectual engagement best serves this temperament.

**Cautions:** The danger of this combination is that the Melancholic element can deepen into despondency when the Sanguine warmth is suppressed — and the Sanguine element can become erratic or dissipated when the Melancholic depth is neglected. The wisest approach is to cultivate both: warmth and social engagement alongside periods of genuine solitary depth.

# CHOLERIC-PHLEGMATIC — Hot & Dry with Cold & Moist

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Choleric-Phlegmatic temperament combines two opposing pairs of qualities — the hot dryness of Choleric and the cold moisture of Phlegmatic. This produces a constitution that is in some degree of internal conflict — heat and cold, dryness and moisture, activity and passivity. The body may reflect a certain unevenness or alternation between vigor and lethargy. The primary health susceptibilities tend to emerge from whichever element is currently dominant — Choleric episodes may produce inflammatory conditions, while Phlegmatic episodes may produce congestion and accumulation of fluid. The internal tension of this combination can itself be a source of health strain if not properly managed.

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**Governing principle:** The opposing qualities of this combination produce an inherent internal tension that requires active management. The native benefits from attending carefully to which element is currently dominant and counteracting the excess of that element.

**Climate:** Temperate conditions that avoid both extremes. Neither hot and dry climates (which inflame the Choleric element) nor cold and damp climates (which compound the Phlegmatic element) are beneficial.

**Diet:** A temperate diet that avoids extremes — neither heavily spiced and roasted (Choleric) nor cold and heavy (Phlegmatic). Warming without inflaming, and moist without saturating.

**Exercise:** Moderate and consistent exercise, avoiding the intense competitive engagement that the Choleric element may crave (which inflames) and the complete inactivity that the Phlegmatic element may prefer (which accumulates). A regular, moderate practice serves this temperament better than either extreme.

**Cautions:** The alternation between Choleric intensity and Phlegmatic passivity can produce a pattern of overexertion followed by excessive rest — each extreme compounding the opposite element's accumulation. Consistent moderation, rather than cycling between extremes, is the most important single recommendation for this combination.

# CHOLERIC-MELANCHOLIC — Hot & Dry with Cold & Dry

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Choleric-Melancholic temperament is governed by an excess of dryness — both the hot dryness of bile and the cold dryness of black bile produce a constitution of unusual intensity, depth, and potential brittleness. The body tends toward leanness and a certain hardness — the dryness in both elements contracts and firms the tissues. The primary health susceptibility is the compound effect of dryness in two forms — conditions of desiccation, nervous tension, digestive disorders involving the bile, and a tendency toward conditions that are both intense (Choleric) and enduring (Melancholic). Lilly considers this the most inventive of the mixed temperaments: "Cholerick-Melanchollick men have excellent inventions" — the Choleric element provides the force and ambition, and the Melancholic element provides the depth and concentration.

*"Cholerick-melanchollick men have excellent inventions."*
— William Lilly, Christian Astrology, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**Governing principle:** Both elements share dryness — the primary need is for moisture and moderation of the drying influences. The opposition between the Choleric heat and the Melancholic cold requires secondary attention.

**Climate:** Moist conditions are most beneficial for this combination. Spring, with its moisture and warmth, is particularly restorative. Avoid hot and dry climates entirely — they compound both the Choleric and Melancholic dryness simultaneously.

**Diet:** Moist, moderately warming, and nourishing foods — broths, soups, preparations with adequate oil and moisture. Foods that both moisturize and provide substance counteract the dual dryness of this combination without inflaming the Choleric heat.

**Exercise:** Moderate physical activity that includes adequate rest and recovery. The combination of Choleric intensity and Melancholic endurance can produce a native who drives themselves excessively — rest and recovery are as important as the exercise itself. Activities that combine physical engagement with creative or intellectual content serve this temperament particularly well.

**Cautions:** The tendency of this combination toward obsessive concentration and self-driven intensity is both its greatest gift and its primary health risk. Regular rest, adequate moisture in diet and climate, and deliberate periods of lightness and pleasure counteract the drying and hardening tendency of this very productive but potentially rigid combination.

# PHLEGMATIC-MELANCHOLIC — Cold & Moist with Cold & Dry

## Health Tendencies (Free)

The Phlegmatic-Melancholic temperament is governed by an excess of cold — both the cold moisture of phlegm and the cold dryness of black bile produce a constitution of unusual depth, introversion, and susceptibility to cold-related complaints. The body tends toward pallor (Phlegmatic) and leanness or darkness (Melancholic) — the two cold elements together reduce vitality and warmth significantly. The primary health susceptibility is the compound effect of cold in two forms — chronic conditions of accumulation (Phlegmatic) and restriction (Melancholic), conditions involving the spleen, bladder, and digestive system, and a general tendency toward the slow, enduring illnesses that both Saturn-governed temperaments produce. Ptolemy's description of Saturnine diseases — cold bellies, phlegm, rheumatic conditions, meagreness, and chronic weakness — applies with particular force to this combination.

*"Saturn causes his subjects to have cold bellies, increases the phlegm, makes them rheumatic, meagre, weak, jaundiced, and prone to dysentery, coughing, raising, colic."*
— Claudius Ptolemy, Tetrabiblos, Book III

---

## Traditional Recommendations (Pro)

**Governing principle:** Both elements share cold — warmth is the most important single counterbalance for this combination. The secondary distinction between moisture (Phlegmatic) and dryness (Melancholic) is less important than the shared need for warming.

**Climate:** Warm conditions are most beneficial and most essential for this temperament. The Phlegmatic-Melancholic native is the most susceptible of all temperaments to cold and should seek warmth in climate, environment, and lifestyle with particular deliberateness. Summer is the most naturally beneficial season.

**Diet:** Warming, nourishing, and easily digested foods are most important. Spices, warming herbs, and hot preparations address the shared cold of both elements. The native should avoid cold, raw, and heavy foods with particular consistency, as both elements compound the difficulty of digesting cold and heavy preparations.

**Exercise:** Regular, warming physical activity is among the most important practical recommendations for this combination — the shared cold of both elements is most effectively counteracted by the internal warmth generated through physical movement. The Phlegmatic tendency toward rest and the Melancholic tendency toward withdrawal both work against the exercise that this temperament most needs.

**Cautions:** The compounded cold of this combination produces a particular susceptibility to chronic and difficult-to-resolve conditions — both the sluggish accumulation of the Phlegmatic and the slow restriction of the Melancholic resist quick resolution. Consistent warmth, adequate nourishment, and deliberate social engagement counteract the most characteristic dangers of this most inward-turning of the mixed temperaments.
